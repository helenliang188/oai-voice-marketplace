# Style Patterns

Use these patterns to rewrite comms in a way that is direct, spoken, evidence-led, and values-grounded.

## Core Pattern

Strong rewrites usually move in this order:

1. Point: the decision, change, ask, or conclusion.
2. Evidence: the concrete signal that makes the point credible.
3. Value: the principle behind the choice or tradeoff.
4. Next step: what the reader should expect or do.

Do not force all four pieces into every paragraph. Use the pieces the communication actually needs.

## Before and After Patterns

### Replace Setup With the Point

Before:

> I wanted to take a moment to provide an update on the workstream and share some context on where we landed after a number of discussions.

After:

> We are narrowing the workstream to the two launches that matter most this quarter.

Why it works: the reader gets the decision immediately.

### Replace Hype With Proof

Before:

> This will be a really exciting and impactful improvement for the team.

After:

> This should save the team about six hours a week by removing duplicate review steps.

Why it works: the claim is supported by a concrete reason.

### Replace Process With Rationale

Before:

> After several rounds of review with the cross-functional group, we aligned on the next phase.

After:

> We are moving to the next phase because the pilot resolved the two risks we were watching: accuracy and response time.

Why it works: the decision is explained by the evidence, not the meeting path.

### Replace Vague Values With a Real Tradeoff

Before:

> We made this change because we care deeply about quality.

After:

> We are delaying the launch by one week because quality matters more than hitting the original date with known gaps.

Why it works: the value is tied to a concrete choice.

### Replace Corporate Distance With Human Language

Before:

> It is important that stakeholders are informed of the operational implications associated with this transition.

After:

> We want everyone affected by this change to know what is changing, when it happens, and where to get help.

Why it works: the sentence sounds like something a person would say.

## Common Moves

- Lead with "here is what is changing" when the draft buries an update.
- Lead with "we decided" when the draft describes process but hides the decision.
- Lead with "the data shows" when the draft tries to persuade without evidence.
- Lead with "we are choosing X over Y" when the draft needs to explain a tradeoff.
- Cut "excited," "thrilled," "robust," "leverage," "stakeholders," and "alignment" unless they are genuinely the simplest accurate words.

## Questions That Sharpen the Rewrite

Ask these when needed:

- What is the one thing the reader should take away?
- What evidence makes this true?
- What decision or tradeoff are we explaining?
- Which value should guide how readers interpret this decision?
- What should the reader do next?

## Internal Example Distillation

When using internal channels or documents to improve this reference:

- Extract reusable writing patterns only.
- Do not copy message text, names, confidential project details, metrics, or sensitive source material into the skill.
- Prefer paraphrased patterns like "leads with the decision, then gives the customer signal" over source-specific examples.
- Keep the final skill shareable by removing anything that depends on private context.
